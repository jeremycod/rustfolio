# ⚖️ What Is the Risk Comparison Page?

This page answers:

> "Which stock is riskier — and how do they compare?"

Instead of analyzing one position at a time,  
this page lets you compare 2–4 tickers side-by-side.

This is especially useful when:

- Choosing between two investments
- Rebalancing
- Reducing risk
- Evaluating replacements for a stock

Let's explain each in beginner terms.

---

# 📈 Risk Score

Example:
- AAPL: 73.8 (High)
- COL: 36.2 (Low)

Higher score = higher overall risk.

This is an aggregated measure of volatility, drawdowns, and beta.

---

# 📉 Volatility

Shows how much price fluctuates.

Higher volatility:
- Bigger price swings
- More emotional stress
- Greater short-term risk

Lower volatility:
- More stable price movement
- Smoother growth

---

# 📉 Maximum Drawdown

Shows worst historical drop.

If one stock has:
- -80% drawdown
and another:
- -20% drawdown

The first has experienced more severe crashes.

---

# 📊 Beta (vs S&P 500)

Measures how sensitive a stock is to the market.

- Beta = 1 → moves with market
- Beta > 1 → more aggressive
- Beta < 1 → more defensive

If market drops 10%:
- Beta 1.5 stock may drop ~15%
- Beta 0.5 stock may drop ~5%

---

# 📈 Sharpe Ratio

Measures return relative to risk.

Higher Sharpe:
Better reward per unit of risk.

Low Sharpe:
Risk not efficiently compensated by returns.

---

# 📉 VaR (Value at Risk)

Estimates potential loss over a given period at a specific confidence level.

Example:
VaR = -4%

Means:
There is a statistical probability the stock may lose at least 4% in a defined period.

# 🧩 Practical Example

If choosing between:

Stock A:
- High volatility
- High drawdown
- High beta

Stock B:
- Lower volatility
- Lower drawdown
- Moderate beta

Stock B may be better for:
- Conservative investors
- Retirement accounts
- Stability-focused portfolios

Stock A may suit:
- Growth investors
- Higher risk tolerance
- Smaller portfolio allocation

---

# ⚠️ Common Beginner Mistakes

❌ Choosing based only on past return  
❌ Ignoring drawdown history  
❌ Misunderstanding beta  
❌ Comparing without considering position size  

---

# 📌 Important Reminder

Higher risk is not automatically bad.

It depends on:

- Your time horizon
- Your risk tolerance
- Your portfolio structure
- Your diversification level
