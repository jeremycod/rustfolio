# Rustfolio Tutorial – Correlations Page

> Audience: Beginners who want to understand diversification  
> Goal: Learn how assets move relative to each other and why correlation matters for risk control.

---

# 🔗 What Is the Correlations Page?

This page answers:

> "Do my investments move together — or independently?"

Correlation measures how two assets move in relation to each other.

Understanding correlation is one of the most important concepts in portfolio management.

Because:

Diversification is not about owning many stocks.  
It's about owning assets that **don't move the same way**.

---

# 📊 Page Overview

Main components:

- Time Period Selector (e.g., 90 Days)
- Correlation Statistics Summary
- Correlation Heatmap Matrix
- Diversification Score

---

# 📈 Correlation Basics (Beginner Explanation)

Correlation values range from:

- **+1.0** → Move exactly together
- **0.0** → No relationship
- **-1.0** → Move opposite each other

Example:

If two stocks have correlation 0.90:
They move almost the same direction most of the time.

If correlation is -0.30:
They sometimes move in opposite directions.

---

# 📊 Correlation Statistics Panel

The page shows summary metrics such as:

## Average Correlation

Example:

```
6.2%
```

Lower average correlation = better diversification.

---

## Max Correlation

Example:

```
99.7%
```

This shows the most highly correlated pair in your portfolio.

If two assets are near 100% correlated:
They behave almost identically.

---

## High Correlation Pairs

Example:

```

```

This highlights positions that may reduce diversification.

---

## Diversification Score

Example:

```
8.8 / 10
```

Higher score = better diversification.

This is a simplified diversification health indicator.

---

# 🔥 Correlation Heatmap (Main Section)

This is a color-coded matrix comparing each asset against every other asset.

### Color Guide

- 🟢 Green → Positive correlation (move together)
- 🔴 Red → Negative correlation (move opposite)
- Light colors → weaker relationship
- Dark colors → stronger relationship

---

# 🧠 How To Read the Heatmap

Rows and columns represent tickers.

Each cell shows correlation between the two.

Example:

If:
AAPL & MSFT = 0.85  
They move very similarly.

If:
JPM & NVDA = -0.20  
They occasionally move in opposite directions.

---

# ⚠️ Why High Correlation Matters

If you own:

```
5 stocks  
but all have 0.85+ correlation  
```

You are not truly diversified.

You are concentrated in the same risk factor.

---

# 📉 Negative Correlation Is Powerful

If one stock drops,
a negatively correlated asset may rise.

This smooths portfolio volatility.

Common diversification tools:

- Bonds vs equities
- International exposure
- Defensive sectors
- Gold or commodities

# 🧩 Real-World Example

Technology stocks often correlate strongly.

If your portfolio contains:

- AAPL
- MSFT
- NVDA
- AMZN

Even though they are different companies,
they may move similarly during tech market shifts.

That increases systemic risk.

---

# ⚠️ Common Beginner Mistakes

❌ Assuming more stocks = diversified  
❌ Ignoring sector clustering  
❌ Not checking correlation during bull markets  
❌ Forgetting correlations rise during market crashes  

---

# 📌 Important Insight

During market crises:
Correlations often increase.

Assets that usually behave independently
may suddenly move together.

That's why correlation analysis matters.
