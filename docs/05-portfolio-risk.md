# 📉 Core Risk Metrics Explained

## Maximum Drawdown

Example shown:

```
-10.78%
```

This means:
At some point, your portfolio dropped 10.78% from a peak before recovering.

It answers:
> "How bad has it historically gotten?"

---

## Beta

Example:

```
0.01
```

Beta measures sensitivity to the overall market.

- Beta = 1 → moves like the market
- Beta > 1 → more volatile than market
- Beta < 1 → less volatile

A beta of 0.01 means:
Your portfolio barely moves with the market (very low correlation).

---

## Sharpe Ratio

Example:

```
0.65
```

The Sharpe ratio measures:
> Return per unit of risk taken

Higher is better.

- Below 1 = moderate risk efficiency
- Above 1 = good
- Above 2 = excellent

A Sharpe of 0.65 means:
You're getting moderate returns for the risk you're taking.

---

## Volatility

Example:

```
25389.09% Annualized
```

(Note: This appears unusually high in demo data)

Volatility measures:
> How much your portfolio value swings up and down

High volatility = larger price swings  
Low volatility = more stable

### What Risk Contribution Means

Even if a stock is only 10% of your portfolio by value,
it might contribute 30% of total risk.

This happens when:
- The stock is highly volatile
- It correlates poorly with other holdings
- It has extreme price swings

# 🧠 Risk vs Return Balance

Remember:

Higher returns often require higher risk.

The goal is:
> Maximize returns for your acceptable risk level

Not:
> Maximize returns at any cost

---

# 📌 Key Takeaway

Performance tells you:
> "How much money did I make?"

Risk tells you:
> "How much could I lose — and how likely is it?"

Both matter equally for long-term success.
